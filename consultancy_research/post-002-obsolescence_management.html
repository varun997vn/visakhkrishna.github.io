Obsolescence management in railway sector

What is obsolescence and why does it matter?

Jan 6th 2023 4 Min read

In these series of blogposts, I try to look into the obsolescence-related problems from the perspective of asset owners in the railway industry.

What is Obsolescence?

Obsolescence means the the process of no longer being used or needed. However, within the context of an asset owner, it can mean either (or both) of the two below (EN 62404)

  • Transition from availability to unavailability from the original manufacturer
  • Permanent transition from operability to non-functionality of the asset due to external reasons
This is applicable for any asset.

Some examples from daily life

For example, it is hard to replace the battery of a laptop from 10 years ago even though the device might be perfectly fine in all other ways - pushing the laptop into obsolescence. Sometimes external reasons like change in regulations can also make an available product obsolete. For example: cars that do not adhere to new stringent pollution limits or refrigerators not following updated energy ratings. Even brand new products can go unsold in these scenarios.

Most of the time, technological advancement renders the asset obsolete with more advanced upgrades available to buy in the market - leading to a dwindling supply of older spare parts. The asset could also itself become redundant such as iPods, replaced entirely by smartphones. Here, there isnt any scope of even having newer upgrades. Sometimes however, the use-case itself becomes obsolete due to external factors/system effects. CD player is one such example since streaming has changed business models of entertainment completely. Therefore it can be very hard to predict obsolescence, let alone manage it.

Obsolescence in railway

While the above examples are easy to understand the nuances associated with obsolescence to us as consumers, obsolescence can work different time-frames It is different from re-use/recycle, decommissioning, retrofitting is a part of it, reverse engineering needs to be involved, supply chain can affect it, It should be a part of the redesign

Why do you need to manage obsolescence?

If it is old, you can always buy newer updated models. So why bother managing obsolescence? While this train of thought is easy to grasp with assets that have a smaller product life cycle (say for < 10 years), assets that cost beyond a certain threshold are better off finding ways to keep being used. That is also the origin of obsolescence management

Obsolescence management as a practice has been fairly prevalent in some niche sectors where the processes of handling obsolescence has become mature. The first application that comes to mind are military applications where it has become a necessity.
