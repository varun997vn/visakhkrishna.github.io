What is obsolescence & why does it matter?

An introduction to obsolescence management

Jan 6th 2023 4 Min read

In a series of blogposts, I try to look into the obsolescence-related problems from the perspective of asset owners in the railway industry. This marks the first post on the topic.

What is Obsolescence?

Obsolescence means the state of no longer being usable or needed. Specifically within the context of asset management, it can mean either (or both) of the two below (EN 62404)

  • Transition from availability to unavailability from the original manufacturer
  • Permanent transition from operability to non-functionality of the asset due to external reasons

Some examples from daily life

Most of the time, technological advancement renders the asset obsolete with more advanced upgrades available to buy in the market - leading to a dwindling supply of older spare parts. For example, it is hard to replace the battery of a laptop from 10 years ago even though the device might be perfectly fine in all other ways. Sometimes external factors like change in regulations can also make an available product obsolete. For example: cars that do not adhere to new pollution norms or refrigerators not following new energy ratings. Even brand new inventory can go unsold in these scenarios.

The asset could also itself become redundant such as mp3 players that got replaced by smartphones. Here, there isnt any scope of even having newer upgrades. Sometimes, the utility itself becomes obsolete due to external factors/system effects. CD player is one such example. Therefore it can be very hard to predict obsolescence, let alone manage it.

Useful life & End of life (EOL)

A good indicator of whether the asset is headed towards obsolescence is by evaluating its remaining useful life. This is illustrated by the reliability 'bathtub' curve in the figure. While the X axis depicts duration of operation, the Y axis depicts the failure rate. It is desired that the asset stays in the 'useful life phase' as much as possible where

  • the failure rate is minimum and fairly constant
  • planned periodic maintenance is mostly enough to keep the asset functioning
  • component replacements are readily available in the market in the near future
  • asset utilisation is maximised

As the asset reaches its 'end of life', failure rate increases, and require substantial efforts to keep it running. In this 'wear and tear' phase, components that make the asset too start running out and exacerbate the problem.

For smaller assets after a certain point it might be feasible to replace entirely. However for larger assets with longer lifetimes it is sometimes necessary to prolong its remaining useful life by managing the obsolescence instead. This forms the core premise for obsolescence management as a practice.

In addition to the first-order of effects in terms of asset utilisation, it can also lead to higher order effects in terms of sustainability and increased capacity.

Why do you need to manage obsolescence?

"If it is old, you can always buy newer updated models. So why bother managing obsolescence?"

While this train of thought is easy to grasp with assets that have a smaller product life cycle (say for < 5 years), assets that cost beyond a certain threshold are better off finding ways to keep being used.

One of the greatest myths related to obsolescence management is that it is only an End-Of-Life Activity. It is a continuous process and should be applied right from product development stage.

Obsolescence management as a practice has been fairly prevalent in some niche sectors where the processes of handling obsolescence has become mature. The first application that comes to mind are military applications where it has become a necessity.
